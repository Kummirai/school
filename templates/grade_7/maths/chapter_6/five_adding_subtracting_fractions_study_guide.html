Grade 7 Mathematics: Chapter 6

Fractions: Adding and Subtracting Fractions

6.5 Adding and subtracting fractions

Adding and subtracting fractions is a fundamental skill in mathematics. The key rule to remember is that you can only add or subtract fractions that have the same denominator (the same "type" of parts).

Adding and Subtracting Fractions with the Same Denominator

When fractions share the same denominator, simply add or subtract their numerators and keep the denominator the same. Think of it like adding or subtracting objects of the same type: 3 apples + 2 apples = 5 apples. Here, the "apples" are the "parts" (the denominator).

Addition Example:

Calculate: \(\frac{1}{4} + \frac{2}{4}\)

\(\frac{1}{4}\)
+
\(\frac{2}{4}\)
=
\(\frac{3}{4}\)

Solution:

\[ \frac{1}{4} + \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1+2}{4} = \frac{3}{4} \]

Subtraction Example:

Calculate: \(\frac{5}{6} - \frac{2}{6}\)

Solution:

\[ \frac{5}{6} - \frac{2}{6} = \frac{5-2}{6} = \frac{3}{6} \]

Always simplify your answer if possible: \(\frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}\).

Adding and Subtracting Fractions with Different Denominators

When fractions have different denominators, you cannot directly add or subtract them. You must first find a common denominator (preferably the Lowest Common Denominator, LCD) and convert the fractions into equivalent fractions with that common denominator.

Steps:

  1. Find the LCD: Determine the smallest common multiple of the denominators.
  2. Convert to Equivalent Fractions: Rewrite each fraction with the LCD as the new denominator.
  3. Add or Subtract Numerators: Perform the addition or subtraction as you would with fractions having the same denominator.
  4. Simplify: Reduce the resulting fraction to its simplest form if possible.

Addition Example:

Calculate: \(\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3}\)

  1. Find LCD: The LCD of 2 and 3 is 6.
  2. Convert to Equivalent Fractions:
    • \(\frac{1}{2} = \frac{1 \times 3}{2 \times 3} = \frac{3}{6}\)
    • \(\frac{1}{3} = \frac{1 \times 2}{3 \times 2} = \frac{2}{6}\)
  3. Add Numerators: \[ \frac{3}{6} + \frac{2}{6} = \frac{3+2}{6} = \frac{5}{6} \]
  4. Simplify: \(\frac{5}{6}\) is already in simplest form.

So, \(\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{5}{6}\).

Subtraction Example:

Calculate: \(\frac{3}{4} - \frac{1}{6}\)

  1. Find LCD: The multiples of 4 are 4, 8, 12, 16... The multiples of 6 are 6, 12, 18... The LCD of 4 and 6 is 12.
  2. Convert to Equivalent Fractions:
    • \(\frac{3}{4} = \frac{3 \times 3}{4 \times 3} = \frac{9}{12}\)
    • \(\frac{1}{6} = \frac{1 \times 2}{6 \times 2} = \frac{2}{12}\)
  3. Subtract Numerators: \[ \frac{9}{12} - \frac{2}{12} = \frac{9-2}{12} = \frac{7}{12} \]
  4. Simplify: \(\frac{7}{12}\) is already in simplest form.

So, \(\frac{3}{4} - \frac{1}{6} = \frac{7}{12}\).

Working with Mixed Numbers

When adding or subtracting mixed numbers, you can either:

  1. Add/subtract the whole numbers and fractions separately (if the fractional part allows).
  2. Convert mixed numbers to improper fractions, perform the operation, and then convert back to a mixed number.

The second method is often more reliable, especially for subtraction where you might need to "borrow" from the whole number.
