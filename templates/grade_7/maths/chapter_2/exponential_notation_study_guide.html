Exponents: The Exponential Notation

2.2 The Exponential Notation

The exponential notation is a shorthand way of writing repeated multiplication of the same number. Instead of writing a long string of multiplications, we can use exponents.

Understanding the Notation: \(b^n\)

In the expression \(b^n\):

  • \(b\) is called the base. It is the number that is being multiplied.
  • \(n\) is called the exponent (or index or power). It tells us how many times the base is multiplied by itself.

For example, \(2^5\) means \(2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2\). Here, 2 is the base and 5 is the exponent.

Special Cases:

  • Squared: An exponent of 2 means the base is multiplied by itself twice (e.g., \(4^2 = 4 \times 4\)). We say "4 squared".
  • Cubed: An exponent of 3 means the base is multiplied by itself three times (e.g., \(4^3 = 4 \times 4 \times 4\)). We say "4 cubed".
  • Any number to the power of 1: Any number raised to the power of 1 is the number itself (e.g., \(7^1 = 7\)).

Examples:

  • Write \(5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5\) in exponential notation: \(5^4\)
  • Write \(3^6\) as a product of factors: \(3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3\)
  • Calculate \(2^4\): \(2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 16\)
