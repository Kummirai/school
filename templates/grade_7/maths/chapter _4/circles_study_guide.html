Construction of Geometric Figures: Circles are Very Special Figures

4.6 Circles are very special figures

Circles are fundamental shapes in geometry, found everywhere from wheels to planets. Understanding their properties is crucial.

Basic Parts of a Circle

A circle is a set of all points in a plane that are equidistant from a central point. Let's define its key components:

  • Centre: The fixed point from which all points on the circle are equidistant.
  • Radius: The distance from the centre to any point on the circle. All radii of the same circle are equal in length.
  • Diameter: A straight line segment that passes through the centre of the circle and has its endpoints on the circle. The diameter is twice the length of the radius (\(Diameter = 2 \times Radius\)).
  • Circumference: The distance around the circle. It's similar to the perimeter of a polygon.

Using a Compass to Draw Circles

A compass is a geometric tool used to draw circles and arcs. It has two legs, one with a sharp point and the other with a pencil or pen holder.

Steps to draw a circle with a compass:

  1. Set the Radius: Open the compass to the desired radius. This is done by placing the sharp point on the zero mark of a ruler and the pencil point on the desired measurement (e.g., 4 cm for a 4 cm radius).
  2. Place the Centre Point: Place the sharp point of the compass firmly on the paper where you want the centre of your circle to be. This point should not move.
  3. Rotate the Compass: Gently rotate the pencil arm of the compass around the fixed sharp point, allowing the pencil to draw a smooth, continuous circle. Keep even pressure on the pencil, and ensure the compass opening does not change.

Drawing a circle with a compass.

You can draw circles of different sizes by adjusting the distance between the compass legs (the radius).

Circles and Geometric Figures

Circles are often used as construction tools to help draw other geometric figures accurately. For example, you can use circles to construct perpendicular lines or divide lines into equal segments, as often seen in higher-level constructions.
