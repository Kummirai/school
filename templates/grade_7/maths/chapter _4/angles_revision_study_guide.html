Construction of Geometric Figures: Angles Revision

4.1 Angles revision

Angles are fundamental in geometry. They describe the "opening" between two lines or rays that meet at a common point. This section revises key concepts about angles.

What is an Angle?

When two lines (or rays or line segments) point in different directions, we say they are at an angle to each other. The "size" of the angle depends on how "different" their directions are.

  • If directions are almost the same, the angle is **small**.
  • If directions are very different, the angle is **big**.

Parts of an Angle:

  • Arms of the angle: These are the two lines (or rays) that form the angle.
  • Vertex: This is the point where the two arms meet. The plural of "vertex" is "vertices".

An angle consists of two arms meeting at a vertex. The arc shows the angle being considered.

Labelling Angles:

There are several ways to label angles to clearly indicate which angle is being referred to, especially when multiple angles share a vertex.

  • Using a number: A number (e.g., Angle 1) placed inside the arc.
  • Using a dot or star: A small dot or star inside the arc.
  • Using three letters: The middle letter is always the vertex. For example, for an angle with vertex B and arms extending to A and C, you can write \(\hat{B}\) or \(A\hat{B}C\) or \(C\hat{B}A\). The "hat" symbol (\(\hat{}\)) on the letter shows where the angle is.

Angle ABC, vertex at B.

Important Note:

Arrowheads on the lines (arms) mean that the lines keep on going indefinitely. However, **the length of an angle's arms does not change the size of the angle.** Whether the arms are long or short, the angle size stays the same.

There are always two angles at a vertex (an inner and an outer, or reflex, angle), so the arc is important to show which one is being discussed.
