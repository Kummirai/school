Construction of Geometric Figures: Perpendicular and Parallel Lines with Circles

4.7 Constructing perpendicular and parallel lines using only a compass and a ruler

While a protractor is useful for measuring and drawing angles, you can construct perpendicular and parallel lines precisely using only a compass and a ruler. This method relies on the properties of circles.

Recall:

  • Perpendicular Lines: Intersect at a 90-degree angle.
  • Parallel Lines: Never intersect and maintain a constant distance.

Constructing a Perpendicular Line (Perpendicular Bisector)

This method allows you to construct a line perpendicular to a given line segment and passing through its midpoint (a perpendicular bisector).

  1. Draw a Line Segment: Start with a line segment, say AB.
  2. Draw Arcs from Endpoints: Set your compass to an opening greater than half the length of AB. Place the compass point on A and draw an arc above and below AB.
  3. Repeat from Other Endpoint: Without changing the compass opening, place the compass point on B and draw another arc that intersects the first two arcs. Label the intersection points C and D.
  4. Draw the Perpendicular Line: Use your ruler to draw a straight line connecting points C and D. This line CD will be perpendicular to AB and will bisect AB (cut it in half).

Constructing a perpendicular bisector using a compass and ruler.

You can use a similar method to construct a perpendicular line from a point not on the line, or a perpendicular line at a point on the line.

Constructing Parallel Lines Using Circles (Equidistant Method)

This method leverages the idea that parallel lines are always the same distance apart. It's similar to the method discussed in section 4.5 but explicitly uses the concept of 'equidistant points' through circle arcs.

  1. Draw a Line and a Point: Draw a line XY and mark a point A not on the line.
  2. Draw a Perpendicular from the Point to the Line: Construct a perpendicular line from point A to line XY. Let the intersection be point C. (You can use the perpendicular construction method mentioned above or a protractor for this initial perpendicular.)
  3. Measure the Distance: Measure the length of the segment AC. This is the perpendicular distance from point A to line XY.
  4. Mark Another Point: Choose another point on line XY (e.g., point D). Construct a perpendicular line at D (or use a protractor if allowed). Using your compass, measure the distance AC and mark off this same distance on the perpendicular line from D, creating a new point B.
  5. Draw the Parallel Line: Connect points A and B with your ruler. The line AB will be parallel to XY.

Another way to visualize this using circles involves drawing multiple circles of the same radius:

  1. Draw a Line and Mark Points: Draw a straight line. Mark several points along this line (e.g., P, Q, R).
  2. Draw Circles: Set your compass to a chosen radius (e.g., 3 cm). Place the compass point on P and draw a circle. Without changing the radius, move to Q and draw another circle, then to R and draw a third circle.
  3. Identify Tangent Points: The circles will all touch a line parallel to the original line at points directly above or below their centers. You can draw a line connecting these highest/lowest points (tangent to all circles). This line will be parallel to your original line.

This method clearly demonstrates that parallel lines maintain a constant distance apart.
