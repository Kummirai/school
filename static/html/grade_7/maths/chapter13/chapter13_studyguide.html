13.1 Describing and doing computations

Key Concepts
  • Constant: A quantity that remains the same or fixed in a given situation or formula.
  • Variable: A quantity that can change or vary. It is often represented by a letter symbol (e.g., $x$, $n$, $l$, $w$).
  • Algebraic Expression: A combination of constants, variables, and mathematical operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division).
  • Flow Diagram: A visual representation of a computational instruction, showing the input, operations, and output.
  • Input Number: The starting value in a computation or flow diagram.
  • Output Number: The result obtained after applying the computational instruction to the input number.
Understanding Patterns and Rules

Example: Circles in Rows (from Textbook Question 1)

If diagrams have 2 rows of circles, and the number of circles per row changes, we can find the total number of circles. If 'n' is the number of circles per row, the rule to calculate the total number of circles is $2 \times n$.

Example: If Diagram 11 has 11 circles per row and 2 rows, how many total circles?

Solution: Using the rule $2 \times n$, substitute $n=11$.

$2 \times 11 = 22$ circles.

Example: Odd Number Sequence (from Textbook Question 2)

The sequence 1; 3; 5; 7; 9; ... can be described by the rule $2 \times n - 1$, where 'n' represents the position of the odd number in the sequence.

Example: What is the 10th odd number?

Solution: Using the rule $2 \times n - 1$, substitute $n=10$.

$2 \times 10 - 1 = 20 - 1 = 19$.

Writing Algebraic Expressions

Algebraic expressions are a shorthand for computational instructions.

  • "Add 4 to the input number, then multiply by 5" can be written as $(x+4)\times5$. Here, $x$ is the input number (variable), and 4 and 5 are constants.

Example: Write the abbreviation for "Multiply the input number by 6 and subtract 2."

Solution: Let the input number be $x$. The expression is $6 \times x - 2$ or $6x - 2$.

Example: Describe the computational instruction for $x \rightarrow \times4 \rightarrow +7$.

Solution: Multiply the input number by 4 and then add 7.

13.2 Relationships represented in formulae

Understanding Variables and Constants in Formulae

A formula is a mathematical rule that shows the relationship between different quantities, often expressed using variables and constants.

Example: Perimeter and Area of a Rectangle (from Textbook Question 1)

  • Perimeter formula: $P = 2 \times l + 2 \times b$ or $P = 2 \times (l+b)$
  • Area formula: $A = l \times b$

In the formula $P = 2 \times (l+b)$:

  • The number 2 is a constant because it always represents the two pairs of sides in a rectangle.
  • The letter symbols $l$ (length) and $b$ (breadth) are variables because their values can change depending on the specific rectangle.

Example: Calculate the perimeter and area of a rectangle with length ($l$) = 8 and breadth ($b$) = 4.

Solution:

Perimeter: $P = 2 \times (8+4) = 2 \times 12 = 24$ units.

Area: $A = 8 \times 4 = 32$ square units.

Example: Sindi's Father's Age (from Textbook Question 2)

Formula: $F = x + 37$, where $F$ is the father's age and $x$ is Sindi's age.

In this formula:

  • $x$ (Sindi's age) is the variable.
  • 37 is the constant (the age difference).

Example: If Sindi was 43 years old, how old was her father?

Solution: Substitute $x=43$ into the formula:

$F = 43 + 37 = 80$ years old.

Example: Jacob's Cell Phone Savings (from Textbook Question 3)

Formula: $y = 45 + 5 \times w$, where $y$ is the amount saved and $w$ is the number of weeks.

In this formula:

  • $w$ (number of weeks) and $y$ (amount saved) are variables.
  • 45 (initial savings) and 5 (savings per week) are constants.

Example: Will Jacob have R90 by the 8th week?

Solution: Substitute $w=8$ into the formula:

$y = 45 + 5 \times 8 = 45 + 40 = 85$.

Jacob will have R85 by the 8th week. Since the phone costs R90, he will not have saved enough money.
