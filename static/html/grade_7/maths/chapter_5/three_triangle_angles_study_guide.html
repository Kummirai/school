Geometry of 2D Shapes: Angles in a Triangle

5.3 Angles in a triangle

One of the most fundamental properties of a triangle relates to its angles. No matter the type or size of a triangle, the sum of its interior angles is always the same.

The Angle Sum Property of Triangles

The sum of the interior angles of any triangle is always \(180^\circ\).

This property is a cornerstone of geometry and allows us to find unknown angles within a triangle if we know the other two.

For any triangle, Angle A + Angle B + Angle C = \(180^\circ\).

How to Verify the Angle Sum Property (Activity from Textbook, Page 5 of Chapter 5)

You can verify this property with a simple activity:

  1. Draw a Triangle: Draw any triangle (e.g., an acute, obtuse, or right-angled triangle) on a piece of paper.
  2. Label the Angles: Label the three interior angles A, B, and C.
  3. Cut Out the Triangle: Carefully cut out the triangle.
  4. Tear Off the Corners: Tear off each of the three corners (angles) of the triangle.
  5. Arrange the Corners: Place the three torn corners together so that their vertices meet at a single point and their sides touch.

Observation: You will notice that the three angles fit together perfectly to form a straight line. A straight line forms an angle of \(180^\circ\). This visually demonstrates that the sum of the angles in a triangle is \(180^\circ\).

Visual proof: Angles of a triangle form a straight line when combined.

Using the Angle Sum Property to Find Unknown Angles

If you know two angles of a triangle, you can always find the third by subtracting the sum of the known angles from \(180^\circ\).

Formula: \(Unknown\ Angle = 180^\circ - (Angle\ 1 + Angle\ 2)\)

Example:

In a triangle, two angles measure \(50^\circ\) and \(70^\circ\). Find the third angle.

Solution:
Sum of known angles = \(50^\circ + 70^\circ = 120^\circ\)
Third angle = \(180^\circ - 120^\circ = 60^\circ\)
