5.1 Triangles, quadrilaterals, circles and others

This section introduces fundamental two-dimensional (2D) shapes and their basic properties. We will learn to identify and differentiate between triangles, quadrilaterals, and circles, and explore some of their angle properties.

Basic 2D Shapes

  • Triangle: A closed figure with three straight sides and three angles.
  • Quadrilateral: A closed figure with four straight sides and four angles.
  • Circle: A round figure where every point on its edge is always at the same distance from its centre.

Visual examples of a triangle, quadrilateral, and circle.

Identifying Shapes

To identify shapes, count their straight sides and angles. For circles, look for a perfectly round edge equidistant from a center.

Angles in Triangles and Quadrilaterals

Angles within shapes can be acute (less than \(90^\circ\)), right (exactly \(90^\circ\)), or obtuse (greater than \(90^\circ\) but less than \(180^\circ\)).

  • A triangle can have:
    • Three acute angles.
    • One obtuse angle (and two acute angles).
    • One right angle (and two acute angles).
    • It cannot have two obtuse angles or two right angles, as the sum of angles in a triangle is always \(180^\circ\).
  • A quadrilateral can have:
    • Two obtuse angles.
    • Four right angles (e.g., a square or rectangle).

Sides of Quadrilaterals: Opposite and Adjacent

In a quadrilateral, sides can be described by their relationship to each other:

  • Opposite sides: Sides that are directly across from each other and do not share a common vertex.
  • Adjacent sides: Sides that meet at a common vertex (corner point).

In quadrilateral ABCD, BC and AD are opposite sides. AB and BC are adjacent sides.
