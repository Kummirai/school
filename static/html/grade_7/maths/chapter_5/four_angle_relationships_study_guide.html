Geometry of 2D Shapes: Vertically Opposite Angles, Angles on a Straight Line and Angles Around a Point

5.4 Vertically opposite angles, angles on a straight line and angles around a point

Understanding the relationships between angles formed by intersecting lines and around a point is fundamental in geometry. These relationships help us find unknown angles and solve more complex geometric problems.

Angles on a Straight Line (Angles on a Straight Line Add up to 180°)

When two or more angles lie on a straight line, their sum is always \(180^\circ\).

If angles x and y are on a straight line, then \(x + y = 180^\circ\).

These angles are often called a **linear pair** or **supplementary angles** if they add up to \(180^\circ\).

Angles Around a Point (Angles Around a Point Add up to 360°)

When several angles share a common vertex and together they form a full circle, their sum is always \(360^\circ\).

If angles x, y, and z are around a point, then \(x + y + z = 360^\circ\).

Vertically Opposite Angles (Vertically Opposite Angles are Equal)

When two straight lines intersect, they form two pairs of vertically opposite angles. Vertically opposite angles are always equal in measure.

In the diagram, angle 'a' is vertically opposite to angle 'c', so \(a = c\). Angle 'b' is vertically opposite to angle 'd', so \(b = d\).

Notice that adjacent angles formed by intersecting lines (like 'a' and 'b', or 'a' and 'd') will form angles on a straight line and thus sum to \(180^\circ\).

Applying Angle Relationships to Solve Problems

We can use these rules to find unknown angles in various diagrams.

Example 1: Angles on a Straight Line

If two angles on a straight line are \(x\) and \(70^\circ\), find \(x\).

Solution:
\(x + 70^\circ = 180^\circ\) (Angles on a straight line)
\(x = 180^\circ - 70^\circ\)
\(x = 110^\circ\)

Example 2: Vertically Opposite Angles and Angles on a Straight Line

If two lines intersect and one angle is \(40^\circ\), find the other three angles.

Solution: Let the angles be a, b, c, d, where 'a' is \(40^\circ\).

  • Angle opposite 'a' (let's say 'c') is \(40^\circ\) (Vertically opposite angles are equal).
  • Angle adjacent to 'a' (let's say 'b') forms a straight line with 'a':
    \(b + 40^\circ = 180^\circ\)
    \(b = 140^\circ\) (Angles on a straight line).
  • Angle opposite 'b' (let's say 'd') is also \(140^\circ\) (Vertically opposite angles are equal).
The angles are \(40^\circ, 140^\circ, 40^\circ, 140^\circ\).
