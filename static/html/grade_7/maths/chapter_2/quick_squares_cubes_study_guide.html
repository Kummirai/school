Exponents: Quick Squares and Cubes

2.1 Quick Squares and Cubes

In this section, we will learn about squares and cubes of numbers. Understanding these concepts is fundamental to working with exponents.

Squares

When you multiply a number by itself, the result is called the square of that number. We often say "squared" instead of "times itself". For example, "ten times ten" can be said as "ten squared".

The notation for squaring a number is to write a small '2' as a superscript after the number. For example, \(10 \times 10\) is written as \(10^2\).

Examples:

  • \(2 \times 2 = 4\), so 4 is the square of 2, or \(2^2 = 4\).
  • \(5 \times 5 = 25\), so 25 is the square of 5, or \(5^2 = 25\).
  • \(8 \times 8 = 64\), so 64 is the square of 8, or \(8^2 = 64\).

Cubes

When you multiply a number by itself three times, the result is called the cube of that number. We often say "cubed" instead of "times itself three times". For example, "ten times ten times ten" can be said as "ten cubed".

The notation for cubing a number is to write a small '3' as a superscript after the number. For example, \(10 \times 10 \times 10\) is written as \(10^3\).

Examples:

  • \(2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8\), so 8 is the cube of 2, or \(2^3 = 8\).
  • \(5 \times 5 \times 5 = 125\), so 125 is the cube of 5, or \(5^3 = 125\).
  • \(10 \times 10 \times 10 = 1000\), so 1000 is the cube of 10, or \(10^3 = 1000\).
