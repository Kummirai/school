1.3 Multiples of Powers of Ten

Understanding multiples of 10, 100, 1000, and 10000 is fundamental for quick calculations and a deeper understanding of place value. When you multiply a number by 10, 100, 1000, or any power of ten, you essentially shift the digits to the left and add zeros to the end.

The Rule of Zeros:

When multiplying a whole number by 10, 100, 1000, etc., you simply write the original number and then add the same number of zeros as there are in the multiplier (10, 100, 1000).

Examples:

  • To multiply by 10 (one zero): \(7 \times 10 = 70\) (add one zero)
  • To multiply by 100 (two zeros): \(25 \times 100 = 2500\) (add two zeros)
  • To multiply by 1000 (three zeros): \(12 \times 1000 = 12000\) (add three zeros)
  • To multiply by 10000 (four zeros): \(3 \times 10000 = 30000\) (add four zeros)

This concept also extends to division: when dividing by 10, 100, 1000, you remove zeros from the end of the number (or shift the decimal point to the left).
